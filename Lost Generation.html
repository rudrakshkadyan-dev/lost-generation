Lost Generation: Writers & Themes (1920s)

Overview

The Lost Generation refers to a group of writers in the post-World War I era who expressed disillusionment with contemporary values, embraced experimentation, and often lived as expatriates in Europe — especially Paris.

• Alienation & Disillusionment — sense of cultural rootlessness after the war
• Expatriation — many authors lived abroad to escape materialism at home
• Formal Innovation — minimalist prose, fragmentation, stream of consciousness

Notable Writers

  • Ernest Hemingway — The Sun Also Rises; A Farewell to Arms
  • F. Scott Fitzgerald — The Great Gatsby; Tender Is the Night
  • Gertrude Stein — Influential modernist mentor & writer
  • John Dos Passos — Manhattan Transfer; U.S.A. trilogy
  • T. S. Eliot — Poet of The Waste Land (modernist icon)
  • E. E. Cummings — Unconventional poems and typographic play
  • Sherwood Anderson — Winesburg, Ohio; mentor to younger writers

Note: Many of these figures overlapped with wider modernist movements in art and poetry.

Style & Techniques

• Minimalist Prose — Hemingway's short declarative sentences
• Fragmentation — reflecting fractured modern life
• Multiple Perspectives — using varied narrators and collage-like structures

These techniques helped mirror the confusion and rapid social change of the early 20th century.
